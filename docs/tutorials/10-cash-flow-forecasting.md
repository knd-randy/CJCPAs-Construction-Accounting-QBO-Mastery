# Cash Flow Forecasting for Construction Companies

## Overview

> **"More contractors fail from lack of cash than lack of profit."**

This is the most important truth in construction finance. A company can be profitable on paper and still go bankrupt if it can't pay Friday's payroll. This guide teaches you how to forecast, monitor, and manage cash flow in QuickBooks Online.

---

## Why Construction Cash Flow Is Uniquely Challenging

### The Construction Cash Flow Problem

```
THE CONSTRUCTION CASH FLOW GAP

Week 1     Week 2     Week 3     Week 4     Week 5     Week 6     Week 7     Week 8
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

COSTS GO OUT:
╔═══════╗
║ Labor ║──►Pay weekly
╚═══════╝
         ╔════════════╗
         ║ Materials  ║──►Pay Net 30 (or COD)
         ╚════════════╝
                  ╔═══════╗
                  ║ Subs  ║──►Pay after their work + your collection
                  ╚═══════╝

CASH COMES IN:
                                              ╔═════════════════════════╗
                                              ║ Progress Bill Submitted ║
                                              ╚═════════════════════════╝
                                                            │
                                                     30-45 days
                                                            │
                                                            ▼
                                                    ╔═══════════════╗
                                                    ║ Payment Rec'd ║
                                                    ╚═══════════════╝
                                                            │
                                                      - 10% Retention
                                                            │
                                                            ▼
                                                    ╔═══════════════╗
                                                    ║   NET CASH    ║
                                                    ╚═══════════════╝

THE GAP: You pay costs in Weeks 1-4, receive 90% of revenue in Week 7-8
RETENTION: The final 10% comes months later (if ever)
```

### Construction-Specific Cash Flow Killers

| Cash Flow Challenge | Why It Happens | Impact |
|---------------------|----------------|--------|
| **Retention** | 5-10% held until project completion | Large receivable you can't collect |
| **Front-loaded costs** | Mobilization, materials before billing | Cash out before cash in |
| **Slow pay cycles** | 30-45+ day payment terms | Extended float requirement |
| **Disputed invoices** | Change order conflicts | Delayed or reduced payment |
| **Seasonal work** | Weather-dependent revenue | Uneven cash flow |
| **Bonding requirements** | Cash needed for bond premiums | Cash tied up in bonds |
| **Payroll timing** | Weekly pay vs. monthly collections | Constant cash drain |

## The 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast

### Why 13 Weeks?

- **One quarter** of visibility
- **Weekly granularity** for payroll timing
- **Long enough** to see problems coming
- **Short enough** to forecast accurately
- **Industry standard** for construction and banking

### Key Components Explained

#### Cash Receipts Sources

| Receipt Type | How to Forecast | Timing Considerations |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Progress billing** | Based on billing schedule and collection history | Add 30-45 days from invoice date |
| **T&M/Service** | Based on weekly billing patterns | Usually faster collection (7-14 days) |
| **Retention release** | Per contract terms at substantial completion | Often 30-60 days after completion |
| **Change orders** | Only when approved AND billed | May have different payment terms |
| **Other income** | Equipment sales, interest, etc. | As expected |

#### Cash Disbursements Categories

| Disbursement Type | Timing | Forecasting Method |
|-------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Payroll** | Weekly (usually Friday) | Headcount × average pay |
| **Payroll taxes** | Semi-monthly or monthly | ~30% of gross payroll |
| **Materials** | Per vendor terms | Based on job schedules + Net 30 |
| **Subcontractors** | After your collection | Tied to project billing receipt |
| **Equipment rental** | Monthly | Per rental agreements |
| **Insurance** | Monthly or quarterly | Per policy schedules |
| **Rent/utilities** | Monthly | Fixed amounts |
| **Loan payments** | Per loan schedule | Fixed amounts |
| **Owner distributions** | As needed/planned | Discretionary |

## Setting Up Cash Flow Tracking in QBO

### Step 1: Know Your Current Cash Position

**HOW to See Cash Position in QBO:**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Balance Sheet"
2. Set date to "Today"
3. Look at **Cash and Cash Equivalents** section:
   - Operating checking
   - Payroll checking
   - Savings
   - Money market
   - Petty cash

**Create a Cash Position Dashboard:**

```
DAILY CASH POSITION - November 27, 2024

Account                      Balance        Available*
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Operating Checking          $98,500         $98,500
Payroll Checking            $45,000         $45,000
Savings                     $25,000         $25,000
Line of Credit Available         -          $50,000
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL CASH                 $168,500        $218,500

*Available includes unused credit facilities

COMMITMENTS DUE THIS WEEK
  Friday Payroll            $(22,000)
  Vendor payments due       $(12,500)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net Available After Commits $184,000
```

### Step 2: Track Receivables Timing

**HOW to Age Your Receivables:**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Accounts Receivable Aging Summary"
2. Customize to show by Customer/Project
3. Note the aging buckets: Current, 1-30, 31-60, 61-90, 90+

**WHY This Matters for Cash Flow:**

| Aging Bucket | Collection Probability | Cash Flow Impact |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------|
| Current (0-30) | 95%+ | Include in Week 2-4 forecast |
| 31-60 days | 85% | Include with risk adjustment |
| 61-90 days | 70% | Investigate before including |
| 90+ days | 50% or less | Exclude or heavily discount |

### Step 3: Track Payables Timing

**HOW to Age Your Payables:**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Accounts Payable Aging Summary"
2. Customize to show by Vendor
3. Note due dates and terms

**Cash Disbursement Rules:**

| Vendor Type | Payment Strategy | Cash Flow Impact |
|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| Critical suppliers | Pay on time | Maintain relationships |
| Subcontractors | Pay after collection | Match to receipts |
| Utilities | Auto-pay | Predictable |
| Non-critical | Use full terms | Preserve cash |

### Step 4: Create QBO Reports for Forecasting

**Report 1: Open Invoices**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Open Invoices"
2. Shows all outstanding AR by customer
3. Use invoice dates to estimate collection timing

**Report 2: Unpaid Bills**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Unpaid Bills"
2. Shows all outstanding AP by vendor
3. Use due dates for disbursement forecast

**Report 3: P&L by Project**

1. Go to **Reports** → "Profit and Loss by Customer"
2. Understand which projects drive cash

## Building Your 13-Week Forecast

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Week 1: Initial Setup

**1. Create the Template:**

```
Download or create a 13-week cash flow template with:
- Row for beginning cash
- Section for receipts (detail by project/source)
- Section for disbursements (detail by type)
- Calculated rows for totals, net flow, ending cash
- Target cash threshold row
```

**2. Enter Beginning Cash:**
- Pull current bank balances from QBO
- Enter as Week 1 beginning cash

**3. Enter Known Disbursements:**
- Weekly payroll amounts
- Monthly fixed costs (rent, insurance, loans)
- Payroll tax deposit dates
- Known vendor payments due

#### Week 2: Forecast Receipts

**1. List All Outstanding Invoices:**
- Export Open Invoices report from QBO
- Sort by expected payment date

**2. Apply Collection Assumptions:**

```
COLLECTION TIMING ASSUMPTIONS

Customer Type          Terms     Expected Collection
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
General Contractors    Net 30    Invoice Date + 35-45 days
Developers             Net 30    Invoice Date + 45-60 days
Government             Net 45    Invoice Date + 60-75 days
Commercial Direct      Net 30    Invoice Date + 30-35 days
Residential            Due on Receipt  Invoice Date + 7-14 days
```

**3. Place Receipts in Appropriate Weeks:**
- Based on invoice date + expected collection days
- Consider historical patterns by customer

**4. Add Anticipated New Billings:**
- Estimate next month's progress billings
- Add collection timing (e.g., December billing → January collection)

#### Week 3: Forecast Variable Disbursements

**1. Materials:**
- Based on job schedules and purchase needs
- Consider vendor terms (Net 30, COD, etc.)

**2. Subcontractors:**
- Typically pay after you collect
- Match to expected project receipts

**3. Equipment:**
- Per rental schedules
- Planned equipment purchases

#### Week 4: Review and Adjust

**1. Calculate Each Week:**
- Beginning Cash + Receipts - Disbursements = Ending Cash

**2. Identify Problem Weeks:**
- Where ending cash < minimum threshold
- Where net cash flow is severely negative

**3. Stress Test:**
- What if a major payment is delayed 2 weeks?
- What if a big expense hits unexpectedly?

## The Minimum Cash Threshold

### How Much Cash Do You Need?

**Rule of Thumb:**

```
MINIMUM CASH = 2-4 Weeks of Fixed Operating Costs

Fixed Operating Costs (Weekly):
  Payroll                    $22,000
  Payroll taxes (averaged)    $6,600 ÷ 4 = $1,650
  Rent/utilities (averaged)   $2,500 ÷ 4 = $625
  Insurance (averaged)        $8,300 ÷ 4 = $2,075
  Loan payment (averaged)     $5,000 ÷ 4 = $1,250
  Other fixed                 $1,000
  ───────────────────────────────────────────
  Weekly Fixed Costs         $28,600

MINIMUM CASH (3 weeks) = $28,600 × 3 = $85,800

Rounded: $75,000 - $90,000 minimum
```

### Factors That Increase Minimum

| Factor | Impact | Adjusted Minimum |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| Seasonal business | Higher during slow season | +2 weeks coverage |
| Government work | Slower payment cycles | +50% minimum |
| Large project concentration | Single collection risk | +1-2 weeks coverage |
| Growth phase | Higher working capital needs | +50-100% minimum |
| Line of credit available | Can use credit as backup | May reduce minimum |

## Cash Flow Improvement Strategies

### Speed Up Collections

| Strategy | How to Implement | Impact |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| **Bill immediately** | Invoice same day work is certified | Collect 7-14 days sooner |
| **Electronic invoicing** | Email invoices, not mail | Collect 5-7 days sooner |
| **Early payment discounts** | Offer 2% Net 10 | Trade margin for speed |
| **Retainage billing** | Bill retention promptly at completion | Recover held cash |
| **Progress bill more often** | Monthly → bi-weekly if contract allows | More frequent cash in |
| **Collections follow-up** | Call at Day 25, not Day 45 | Reduce aging |

### Slow Down Disbursements

| Strategy | How to Implement | Impact |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| **Use full payment terms** | If Net 30, pay Day 28-30 | Keep cash longer |
| **Negotiate better terms** | Ask for Net 45 from suppliers | Extended float |
| **Consolidated purchasing** | Fewer orders = fewer payments | Simplified outflow |
| **Align sub payments to collections** | Pay subs after you collect | Match inflows/outflows |
| **Stagger large purchases** | Split orders when possible | Smooth cash outflow |

### Access Additional Cash

| Strategy | When to Use | Considerations |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Line of credit** | Short-term gaps | Interest cost, must repay |
| **Equipment financing** | Large purchases | Preserves cash |
| **Factor receivables** | Emergency cash | Expensive (2-5% fee) |
| **Negotiate progress billing** | Before project start | Get contract terms improved |
| **Retain earnings** | Ongoing | Reduce distributions |

### Reduce Cash Needs

| Strategy | How to Implement | Impact |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| **Reduce retention given** | Negotiate with GCs | Less cash held |
| **Just-in-time materials** | Order as needed | Less inventory cash |
| **Rent vs. buy equipment** | For infrequent use | Preserve cash |
| **Subcontract more** | For specialty work | Pay after you collect |

## Cash Flow Warning Signs

### Early Warning Indicators

| Warning Sign | What It Means | Action |
|--------------|---------------|--------|
| **Stretching payables** | Paying later than terms | Review cash position |
| **AR aging increasing** | Collections slowing | Intensify collection effort |
| **Using LOC frequently** | Operations not self-funding | Review pricing, costs |
| **Skipping owner distributions** | Cash too tight | Assess profitability |
| **Delaying payroll taxes** | Severe cash crisis | Immediate action needed |
| **Vendor complaints** | Reputation at risk | Communicate and prioritize |

### The Cash Flow Death Spiral

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    CASH FLOW PROBLEMS       │
                    │    (Slow collections,       │
                    │     high costs)             │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    DELAYED PAYMENTS         │
                    │    TO VENDORS/SUBS          │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    SUPPLIERS DEMAND         │
                    │    COD OR PREPAYMENT        │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    CASH NEEDS INCREASE      │
                    │    (Can't use credit)       │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    WORK SLOWS DOWN          │
                    │    (Can't buy materials)    │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │    REVENUE DECREASES        │
                    │    (Less to bill)           │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                                   │
                                   ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
                    │        WORSE CASH           │──────────┐
                    │        FLOW PROBLEMS        │          │
                    └─────────────────────────────┘          │
                                   ▲                         │
                                   │                         │
                                   └─────────────────────────┘
                                         REPEAT
```

**Breaking the Spiral:**
1. Communicate with vendors early
2. Prioritize critical suppliers
3. Accelerate collections aggressively
4. Cut non-essential spending
5. Seek bridge financing
6. Consider invoice factoring as last resort

## Weekly Cash Flow Review Process

### Monday Morning Cash Meeting

**Agenda (15-20 minutes):**

1. **Current Position** (2 min)
   - Review actual bank balances
   - Compare to last week's forecast

2. **This Week's Expected Receipts** (3 min)
   - What collections are expected?
   - Any delays or issues?

3. **This Week's Required Disbursements** (3 min)
   - Payroll amount confirmed?
   - Critical vendor payments?

4. **Update 13-Week Forecast** (5 min)
   - Roll forward one week
   - Add Week 14
   - Adjust any changes

5. **Identify Action Items** (5 min)
   - Collections to follow up
   - Payments to delay if needed
   - Decisions needed

### Monthly Cash Flow Deep Dive

**Monthly Review (30-45 minutes):**

1. **Forecast Accuracy**
   - Compare forecasted vs. actual for past month
   - Identify why variances occurred
   - Improve future forecasting

2. **Trend Analysis**
   - Is cash position improving or declining?
   - Seasonal patterns?
   - Project mix impact?

3. **Upcoming Challenges**
   - Large payments coming due
   - Slow collection periods
   - Equipment purchases planned

4. **Strategy Adjustments**
   - Credit line usage plan
   - Collection priorities
   - Payment prioritization

## Cash Flow and Bonding

### Why Sureties Care About Cash Flow

Your bonding company guarantees your performance. They want to know:
- Can you fund projects until collections arrive?
- Is the company liquid?
- Are you managing cash responsibly?

### Cash Flow Metrics Sureties Review

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities | > 1.3 |
| **Quick Ratio** | (Cash + AR) ÷ Current Liabilities | > 1.0 |
| **Working Capital** | Current Assets - Current Liabilities | Positive |
| **Cash to Revenue** | Cash ÷ Annual Revenue | 5-10% |
| **Days Sales Outstanding** | (AR ÷ Revenue) × 365 | < 45 days |

### Preparing Cash Flow Reports for Sureties

**What to Provide:**

1. **Historical cash flow statements**
   - Past 2-3 years
   - Shows operating, investing, financing

2. **13-week cash forecast**
   - Current outlook
   - Demonstrates planning capability

3. **Bank statements**
   - Verifies reported balances
   - Shows transaction patterns

4. **Line of credit status**
   - Available credit
   - Current utilization

## Common Cash Flow Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Profit with Cash

**Problem:** "We're profitable, so cash should be fine"

**Reality:**
```
PROFIT vs. CASH EXAMPLE

                           P&L            Cash
Revenue billed          $100,000            -
Cash collected               -         $85,000 (less retention, AR)
Costs incurred          ($80,000)           -
Cash paid                    -         ($75,000)
───────────────────────────────────────────────
Profit                   $20,000            -
Cash Flow                    -          $10,000

Profit ≠ Cash
```

### Mistake 2: Not Forecasting Weekly

**Problem:** Monthly forecast misses Friday payroll crunch

**Solution:** Always forecast weekly, especially for payroll timing

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Seasonality

**Problem:** Summer cash funds winter, but not planned

**Solution:** Build cash reserves during busy season

### Mistake 4: Optimistic Collection Assumptions

**Problem:** Assume everyone pays in 30 days

**Solution:** Use historical collection patterns, not invoice terms

### Mistake 5: Forgetting Retention Impact

**Problem:** 10% of revenue is held and ignored

**Solution:** Track retention separately; forecast release dates

### Mistake 6: No Minimum Cash Target

**Problem:** Any positive balance feels "okay"

**Solution:** Establish and maintain minimum cash threshold

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cash flow kills more contractors than lack of profit** - take it seriously
2. **Forecast weekly** - monthly isn't granular enough for payroll timing
3. **Use 13-week rolling forecast** - long enough to see problems, short enough to be accurate
4. **Establish minimum cash threshold** - 2-4 weeks of fixed costs
5. **Review weekly** - cash flow isn't "set and forget"
6. **Speed collections, slow payments** - manage the timing gap
7. **Plan for retention** - it's real money you can't spend yet
8. **Watch early warning signs** - address issues before they spiral
